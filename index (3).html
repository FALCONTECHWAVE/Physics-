Modern Physics

De Broglie Wavelength

Proposed by Louis de Broglie, it states that particles, like electrons, exhibit wave-like properties. The wavelength (λ) is given by the formula:

λ = h / p

where h is Planck's constant and p is the momentum of the particle.

Photoelectric Effect

This phenomenon occurs when light shines on a metal surface, causing the emission of electrons. Einstein explained it using the concept of photons, where the energy of a photon is given by:

E = hf

where f is the frequency of light. The effect demonstrates the particle nature of light.

Matter Waves

Matter waves refer to the wave-like behavior of particles. According to quantum mechanics, every particle has an associated wave function that describes its probability distribution.

Bohr Atomic Theory/Spectrum

Niels Bohr proposed a model of the atom where electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed paths or energy levels. The energy levels are quantized, and transitions between these levels result in the emission or absorption of light, leading to atomic spectra.

Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy is released during nuclear reactions, such as fission (splitting of heavy nuclei) and fusion (combining light nuclei). It is harnessed in nuclear power plants to generate electricity.

Electromagnetic Waves

Maxwell's Equations

These four equations describe how electric and magnetic fields interact and propagate as electromagnetic waves. They are:

  • Gauss's Law for Electricity
  • Gauss's Law for Magnetism
  • Faraday's Law of Induction
  • Ampère-Maxwell Law

EM Spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum encompasses all types of electromagnetic radiation, ranging from radio waves to gamma rays, characterized by their wavelength and frequency.

Semiconductors

Diodes

A diode is a semiconductor device that allows current to flow in one direction only. It is used in rectification, signal modulation, and switching.

Logic Gates

Logic gates are the building blocks of digital circuits. They perform basic logical functions (AND, OR, NOT) and are used in computing and electronic devices.

Units and Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional Analysis

This technique involves checking the dimensions of physical quantities to ensure equations are dimensionally consistent. It helps in converting units and deriving relationships between different physical quantities.

Wave Optics

Young's Double Slit Experiment

This experiment demonstrates the wave nature of light. When light passes through two closely spaced slits, it creates an interference pattern on a screen, showing alternating bright and dark fringes.

Polarization

Polarization refers to the orientation of the oscillations of light waves. It can occur through reflection, refraction, or by passing light through a polarizing filter.

Kinetic Theory of Gases (KTG)

Degree of Freedom

The degree of freedom of a gas molecule refers to the number of independent ways in which it can move. For a monatomic gas, it is 3 (translational motion).

Speed of Gas/Specific Heat

The average speed of gas molecules can be derived from the kinetic theory. Specific heat is the amount of heat required to change the temperature of a unit mass of a substance by one degree Celsius.

Gravitation

Acceleration Due to Gravity

The acceleration experienced by an object due to the gravitational force of the Earth is approximately 9.81 m/s².

Law of Gravitation

Newton's law of universal gravitation states that every mass attracts every other mass with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers:

F = G (m₁m₂ / r²)

Alternating Current

Series RLC Circuit

In a series RLC circuit, a resistor (R), inductor (L), and capacitor (C) are connected in series. The total impedance and phase angle can be calculated to analyze the circuit's behavior under AC conditions.

Measurement Instruments

Vernier Caliper

A precision instrument used to measure internal and external dimensions as well as depths. It consists of a main scale and a sliding vernier scale that allows for more accurate measurements.

Screw Gauge

Also known as a micrometer screw gauge, it is used to measure small dimensions with high precision. It consists of a screw mechanism that converts rotational motion into linear motion, allowing for fine measurements.

Electrostatics

Electric Field/Field Lines

An electric field is a region around a charged object where other charges experience a force. Field lines represent the direction and strength of the electric field, with denser lines indicating stronger fields.

Coulomb's Law/Gauss's Law

Coulomb's Law describes the force between two point charges:

F = k (|q₁q₂| / r²)

where k is Coulomb's constant. Gauss's Law relates the electric flux through a closed surface to the charge enclosed by that surface.

Current Electricity

Combination of Resistance

Resistors can be combined in series or parallel. In series, the total resistance is the sum of individual resistances. In parallel, the total resistance can be found using:

1 / Rtotal = 1 / R₁ + 1 / R₂ + ...

Power

The power (P) consumed in an electrical circuit is given by:

P = IV

where I is the current and V is the voltage.

Heating Effect of Current

This effect, also known as Joule heating, occurs when current passes through a resistor, converting electrical energy into heat. The heat produced is given by:

H = I²Rt

where H is the heat, R is resistance, and t is time.

Electric Instruments

Instruments like ammeters, voltmeters, and multimeters are used to measure current, voltage, and resistance in circuits.

Electromagnetic Induction (EMI)

Magnetic Flux

Magnetic flux (Φ) through a surface is defined as the product of the magnetic field (B) and the area (A) perpendicular to the field:

Φ = B · A · cos(θ)

Faraday's Law

Faraday's Law states that a change in magnetic flux through a circuit induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the circuit:

EMF = -dΦ / dt

Lenz's Law

Lenz's Law states that the direction of induced current will be such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it.

Thermodynamics

First Law of Thermodynamics

This law states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. It can be expressed as:

ΔU = Q - W

where ΔU is the change in internal energy, Q is the heat added to the system, and W is the work done by the system.

Thermodynamic Processes

Various processes include isothermal (constant temperature), adiabatic (no heat exchange), isobaric (constant pressure), and isochoric (constant volume) processes, each with distinct characteristics and equations.
